Issuance Guide — Step-by-Step

Follow a guided flow for issuing or authenticating a selected contract. Steps may vary by jurisdiction.

Mode: Digital + Physical
  1. 1

    Identify Contract Type & Parties

    Digital / Physical

    Confirm the exact contract category, number of parties, governing law, and language (Arabic, English, or bilingual).

    • Verify commercial registration or ID numbers for all parties.
    • Check if a standard government template exists.
    • Decide if notarization or attestation is required.
  2. 2

    Prepare Draft & Validate Terms

    Digital Preferred

    Draft or review the contract with clear obligations, duration, renewal terms, and dispute-resolution clause.

    • Use official templates when provided by ministries or regulators.
    • Validate against local labor, commercial, or real estate laws.
    • Translate if one of the parties does not speak the primary language.
  3. 3

    Choose Digital or Physical Channel

    Omni-channel

    Select whether to finalize via government e-portal, notary, or relevant authority office.

    • For many labor & commercial matters, online portals are available.
    • Real estate and personal status often require in-person attendance.
    • Confirm if video-attestation or e-signature is accepted.
  4. 4

    Pay Official Fees & Submit

    Fees + Submission

    Pay applicable governmental fees online or at the counter, then submit all required documents for review.

    • Keep digital receipts and stamped payment confirmations.
    • Ensure names and ID numbers match exactly across documents.
    • Some procedures require biometric verification or OTP.
  5. 5

    Receive, Register & Store

    Completion

    Once approved, download or collect the final document, then ensure it is safely registered and stored.

    • Save certified copies and digital backups.
    • Record expiry, renewal, or notice periods.
    • Share only through secure, traceable channels.

Digital vs. Physical Issuance

Many jurisdictions support fully digital procedures, but some contracts still require physical presence.

Always verify the latest procedure on official government websites before initiating.

For high-value contracts, involve a qualified lawyer to review and negotiate terms.

Frequently Asked Questions

General guidance only. Always validate with an officially licensed professional or authority.

For simple, standardized contracts (like short-term leases or standard employment contracts), you can often use official templates without a lawyer. However, for high-value, long-term, or complex agreements, involving a qualified lawyer is strongly recommended to reduce risk.

Many countries in the Middle East recognize certified digital signatures, especially when issued through approved identity providers or government portals. However, some procedures (like property transfers or personal status matters) may still require in-person signatures and witnessing.

Most authorities require Arabic versions of foreign documents, attested and translated by certified translators. Check whether you must legalize documents at your embassy and the Ministry of Foreign Affairs before submission.

Most modern systems provide a reference or application number that you can use to track status online or via SMS. Keep payment receipts and submission confirmations, as they are often needed to raise support tickets or inquiries.
